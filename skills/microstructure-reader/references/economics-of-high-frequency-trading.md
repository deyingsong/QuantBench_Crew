# Menkveld Economics Of High-Frequency Trading

Source: Albert J. Menkveld, *The Economics of High-Frequency Trading*.

## Why This Source Matters

Menkveld reviews the economic mechanisms through which speed changes trading.
It rejects a single HFT archetype and separates fast market makers from fast
aggressive traders. Use it to review claims about HFT, latency, fragmentation,
and welfare without collapsing distinct activities into one label.

## HFT Is Heterogeneous

The review distinguishes:

- high-frequency market makers that submit passive orders;
- high-frequency aggressive traders that take liquidity;
- cross-market arbitrageurs connecting fragmented venues;
- traders using speed around news or large orders;
- intermediaries providing rapid completion.

A dataset labeled "HFT" may mix these roles. Results should be disaggregated
by strategy, aggressiveness, instrument, and market state.

## Mechanisms Of Speed

Speed can:

- help market makers update stale quotes and manage inventory;
- improve cross-venue price alignment;
- create or intensify races after public news;
- allow fast traders to adversely select slow limit orders;
- anticipate large-order flow;
- produce quote flickering;
- create productive intermediation chains;
- satisfy demand for faster completion.

Each mechanism has different predictions for spreads, depth, price discovery,
profits, and welfare.

## Ambiguous Effects

Theoretical and empirical findings are conditional. Speed may improve
liquidity when it reduces stale-quote risk, but worsen it when fast traders
have an informational advantage over liquidity suppliers. Exchange speed
upgrades can affect small and large stocks differently. Fragmented markets may
benefit from HFT connectivity while becoming fragile when arbitrage capacity
withdraws.

Do not interpret mixed evidence as noise. It may reveal mechanism or regime
heterogeneity.

## Identification Diagnostics

Strong studies exploit exchange upgrades, entry events, or trader identifiers,
but each design still needs scrutiny:

- Did the event alter fees, matching rules, or capacity as well as speed?
- Are treated and control securities comparable?
- Did traders migrate across venues?
- Is HFT classification stable and behavior-based?
- Are market-quality effects measured during normal and stressed conditions?
- Are profits evidence of productive intermediation, information, or rents?

## Welfare Diagnostics

Private profitability does not imply social value, and tighter spreads do not
settle welfare. Include:

- gains from trade and completion speed;
- information efficiency;
- adverse-selection transfers;
- technology and arms-race costs;
- resilience and crash behavior;
- distribution across retail, institutional, and intermediary traders.

## Reviewer Use

Use this source whenever a paper makes a broad statement about HFT. Force the
claim into a specific mechanism, trader role, venue structure, and regime.
Require evidence that distinguishes speed itself from correlated technology,
strategy, and market-design changes.
