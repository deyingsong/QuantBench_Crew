# Critical Reading And Grounded Future Work

## Transcript-Distilled Method

Academic critical thinking asks how an argument participates in a scholarly
conversation: what it claims, what evidence supports it, what assumptions
connect evidence to conclusion, and what alternative interpretation remains.
Writing guidance adds a useful test: unclear prose often exposes unclear
science or missing evidence.

## Critique Lenses

- `logic`: does the conclusion follow from the evidence?
- `assumptions`: what must be true for the model or argument to hold?
- `internal_validity`: could bias, leakage, confounding, or design choices
  explain the result?
- `construct_validity`: do measurements represent the claimed concept?
- `external_validity`: where, when, or for whom should the result generalize?
- `comparison_validity`: are baselines and alternatives adequate and fair?
- `reproducibility`: are data, methods, and settings specified sufficiently?
- `falsifiability`: what observation would overturn the central claim?

## Author Statements Versus Reader Inference

Keep these separate:

- Author-stated limitations and future work are paper claims.
- Reader-inferred threats are critiques and require explicit reasoning.
- Missing reporting is uncertainty, not proof that the procedure was absent.

Ground each future direction in a limitation:

`limitation or unanswered question -> proposed test or extension -> evidence that would resolve it`

Avoid generic suggestions such as "use more data" unless the missing data
addresses a named validity threat.
