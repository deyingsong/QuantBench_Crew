# Investments

Source: Zvi Bodie, Alex Kane, and Alan J. Marcus, *Investments*.

## Why This Source Matters

This source supplies the broader investment frame linking inflation, real
rates, term structure, business cycles, monetary and fiscal policy, bond
portfolio management, and cross-asset allocation. Use it to test whether a
rates result makes economic sense outside a narrowly fitted curve model.

## Real And Nominal Rates

Nominal rates combine real-rate compensation and expected inflation. TIPS
yields provide a market real-rate reference, while nominal-TIPS breakevens are
not pure inflation forecasts because liquidity and risk premia can differ.

Always distinguish:

- nominal versus real return;
- expected versus realized inflation;
- inflation exposure of assets versus liabilities;
- market-implied versus survey or model expectations.

## Yield Curve Interpretation

The term structure links spot rates, forward rates, holding-period returns,
and theories of expectations and liquidity premiums. Curve slope can contain
information about future short rates and business conditions, but it is not a
mechanical forecast.

Review whether curve signals remain stable after accounting for:

- time-varying term premium;
- policy regimes;
- inflation uncertainty;
- credit and liquidity conditions;
- the sample's business-cycle composition.

## Macroeconomic Analysis

Key macro variables include GDP, employment, inflation, interest rates, and
economic indicators. Fiscal and monetary policy affect demand through
different channels and lags. Business cycles include recession, expansion,
overheating, and stagflation, each with different asset implications.

Avoid a one-dimensional "growth up means yields up" interpretation. The rates
response depends on inflation, policy reaction, supply shocks, credibility,
and risk appetite.

## Monetary Policy

Monetary policy operates largely through interest rates and financial
conditions, but its effects are indirect and lagged. Anticipated and
unanticipated actions differ. Market prices may move before the policy event
because they incorporate expectations.

For event studies, separate:

- policy surprise from announced level;
- expected path from current action;
- information effect from policy shock;
- monetary from fiscal and supply developments;
- announcement from implementation.

## Duration, Convexity, And Immunization

Duration and convexity approximate price sensitivity. Immunization matches
asset and liability duration but requires rebalancing and is most reliable for
small parallel shifts. Cash-flow matching is more direct but constrains asset
selection.

The source explicitly notes:

- conventional duration assumes a flat curve;
- duration matching does not immunize nonparallel moves;
- nominal immunization is inappropriate for inflation-linked liabilities;
- changing rates and passage of time require rebalancing.

## Portfolio And Cross-Asset Context

Macro and rates signals should be evaluated by their contribution to total
portfolio risk, not in isolation. Correlation, diversification, benchmark
choice, and liability structure determine whether a rate exposure is useful.
Cross-asset relationships can change across inflation and business-cycle
regimes.

## Reviewer Diagnostics

- Are nominal and real quantities mixed?
- Does a yield-curve signal survive different term-premium estimates and
  business cycles?
- Is policy surprise measured relative to market expectations?
- Are monetary-policy lags and anticipation addressed?
- Is duration matching incorrectly treated as full immunization?
- Does the strategy hedge the actual liability, including inflation?
- Are cross-asset correlations assumed stable across inflationary and
  recessionary regimes?

## Reviewer Use

Use this source for macro interpretation, business-cycle classification,
inflation, broad portfolio implications, and basic bond management. It provides
the economic plausibility check around more specialized fixed-income models.
