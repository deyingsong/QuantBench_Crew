# Reading Questions And Research Gaps

## Transcript-Distilled Method

The academic transcript corpus repeatedly treats a paper as an argument in a
scholarly conversation. Efficient readers survey first, then spend attention
where the argument is made.

Use this reading order:

1. Title and keywords reveal the declared topic.
2. Abstract and conclusion reveal the claimed question, answer, and value.
3. Figures, tables, and captions reveal what evidence the paper considers
   central.
4. Introduction reveals the field framing, disagreement, gap, and claimed
   contribution.
5. Results and discussion test whether the stated question was actually
   answered.

Strong research writing communicates one important, reusable idea. Reverse
engineer that idea by asking:

- What exactly is the problem?
- Why does solving it matter?
- What does the field currently believe or do?
- What fails, remains unknown, or produces conflicting evidence?
- What answer does this paper claim to contribute?

## Classification

Classify a gap rather than describing it vaguely:

- `evidence_gap`: the answer has not been tested adequately;
- `method_gap`: current methods cannot measure, estimate, or solve it well;
- `scope_gap`: evidence exists only for limited populations, periods, or
  settings;
- `contradiction`: prior findings disagree;
- `theory_gap`: observations lack an adequate explanation;
- `translation_gap`: knowledge exists but has not been implemented or tested
  in practice.

## Evidence Discipline

Treat AI extraction as a first pass. Verify every field against the primary
paper, preserve source locations, and distinguish explicit statements from
Reader inference. A fluent literature summary without citations is not a
defensible field-state assessment.
